What They're Building

A constellation of satellites with large deployable mirrors (~50m x 50m at scale) that reflect sunlight to precise 5km-wide spots on Earth after sunset.³

  • First satellite: EARENDIL-1 — 18m x 18m mirror, 100kg, launches Spring 2026
  • Orbit: Sun-synchronous at ~600km, following the day/night terminator
  • Brightness: Full moon initially → up to 36,000 lux (daylight) at scale
  • 2030 goal: 4,000 satellites; long-term: potentially 250,000

Use Cases

  • Solar energy: Extend solar farm generation into twilight/night hours
  • Disaster response: Illuminate search-and-rescue zones
  • Industrial/Construction: Extend working hours at remote sites
  • Agriculture: Extend growing seasons, boost yields
  • Defense: Uninterrupted solar power for critical operations
  • Urban lighting: Eventually replace streetlights

The Controversy

Heads up: Astronomers are vocal critics.⁶

  • Light pollution: Concerns about disrupting astronomy observations
  • Wildlife: Unknown effects on nocturnal animals and ecosystems
  • Scale skepticism: Some physicists question whether the economics work at the brightness levels needed for meaningful solar power boost

Reflect's Response

  • Light is contained within the spot — not scattered broadly
  • Can turn off in seconds by rotating satellites
  • Will maintain exclusion zones for observatories and sensitive habitats
  • Only provide light when specifically requested and approved by local authorities
"Give us four minutes. If everyone hates it, we can turn it off."
— Tristan Semmelhack, Monocle interview⁷
